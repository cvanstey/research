Data Scientist · Oral Historian · Computational Archivist

Combining spatial data, census records, and machine learning with archival methods and oral history practice to surface the communities most often left out of the picture.

I graduated with a degree in Literature from Stockton University in 2022, then returned to earn a Master of Arts in American Studies in December 2024. As an oral history practitioner, projects have spanned photo restoration, transcription, translation, digital archiving, grant writing, and spatial and predictive modeling.

Featured Research

Predicting Food Access in New Jersey

Presented at the Stockton University Data Science & Strategic Analytics Graduate Symposium, May 2026, with Dr. Robert Olsen.

ZIP-Code Level Food Access, Socioeconomic Conditions & Health Outcomes

Cynthia Anstey & Dr. Robert Olsen · Stockton University · May 2026

This study integrates multiple data sources — OpenStreetMap Overpass API, USDA Food Access Research Atlas, 2021 Census TIGER/Line ZCTA shapefiles, American Community Survey 5-Year Estimates, and CDC PLACES health data — to construct a comprehensive ZIP-code-level picture of food access across New Jersey.

The model classifies each ZIP code as a Food Desert, Food Mirage, Food Swamp, or Healthy/Fragile Access zone, departing from the federal FARA standard in three key ways: it uses ZIP codes rather than census tracts, treats income as a continuous predictor, and applies a uniform 5-mile buffer from ZIP centroids. The swamp ratio formula — (fast food + convenience) ÷ supermarkets — identifies communities where unhealthy outlets significantly outnumber grocers.

A transit buffer analysis of ZIP 08087 (Little Egg Harbor–Stafford Township area) used GTFS feeds and 500-meter buffers around transit stops to map whether residents without cars can actually reach food resources. A key finding: desert status predicts cardiovascular disease and depression, while swamp ratio predicts diabetes and tooth loss.
