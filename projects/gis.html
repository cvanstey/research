Transit Food Access Analysis

GTFS pipeline and automated Word policy brief generator auditing bus stop proximity to grocery stores in NJ ZIP codes — exposing how county-operated routes absent from federal data feeds cause systematic misclassification as food-accessible.

GISGTFSPolicyStockton 2026

What It Does

Takes any NJ ZIP code as input and produces two outputs: a structured JSON file for downstream modeling and a formatted Word policy brief. For each ZIP it loads the ZCTA boundary, clips NJ Transit GTFS stops to that boundary, auto-discovers all routes serving it, computes geodesic walk distances from each stop to every nearby supermarket (via OSM or a sidecar file), and loads county-operated route data from optional JSON sidecars that are not present in the GTFS feed.

Core Finding — ZIP 08087

Little Egg Harbor appears transit-served in every federal and state dataset because NJ Transit Route 559 runs through it. In practice, the nearest Route 559 stop is beyond the 0.25-mile USDA walkability threshold from both Walmart and Acme. The only route that stops directly at grocery store entrances — Ocean County’s OC 6 — operates three days per week, enforces a two-bag limit, and is entirely absent from the NJ Transit GTFS feed. It therefore does not appear in the USDA Food Access Research Atlas, NJ Transit trip planners, or any automated proximity model.

Policy Brief Generator

The Word document is fully data-driven: all distances, headways, shopping windows, risk labels, and recommendations are computed at run time from GTFS schedule data, ML pipeline scores, and county sidecar files. The brief includes a food environment profile (swamp ratio, desert classification, statewide percentile ranks), per-route service statistics, a walk-distance table flagging stores that exceed the walkability threshold, county transit service profiles with shopping window tables, and tiered recommendations.

Libraries

  • geopandas + shapely — ZIP boundary loading, spatial join, stop clipping
  • geopy (geodesic) — straight-line distances consistent with USDA methodology
  • python-docx — programmatic Word doc generation with custom table styles and cell-level formatting
  • pandas + zipfile — GTFS txt parsing, headway computation, service-day extraction
County routes (demand-responsive, shuttle, or fixed-schedule services not in the NJ Transit GTFS feed) are supported via ZIP-level JSON sidecar files, making the pipeline extensible to any county in the state without code changes.

Atlantic City Archive

OCR + GIS reconstruction of historical business networks.

GISOCR
Open Project →

Source Material

1930s Atlantic City Board of Trade tourism brochures documenting African American businesses, leisure venues, and civic life during the Depression and early urban renewal periods — held in fragmented form across a private collection and the Atlantic City Free Public Library (six known brochures in the series).

Approach

Transcription and structured extraction of business listings, addresses, and names from brittle, ephemeral print materials, reconstructed as a geocoded, mappable dataset in QGIS to visualize historical business networks and place them in spatial/civic context.

Part of a Larger Fellowship Project

This work is one strand of a broader applied-AI fellowship project with Stockton University Special Collections, evaluating OCR transcription quality, Dublin Core/MARC metadata tooling, and WCAG 2.2 accessibility compliance across a 40-document archival corpus spanning the 1800s–1940s. A central finding from that work: a 0.53% character error rate (Adobe OCR + correction) is sufficient for human readability and accessibility compliance, but insufficient for downstream computational tasks like named entity recognition or geocoding — where small error rates still break tokenization and string matching. The right question isn't "is this error rate acceptable," but "acceptable for what purpose."

Related write-up in progress: a methods paper on transcription, metadata, and accessibility in special collections archives, including a critique of vendor API paywalls (Preservica) as a structural barrier to ADA/WCAG compliance at under-resourced institutions.
